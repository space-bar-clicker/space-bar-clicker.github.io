Plate Materials Explained: Aluminum, Brass, and Polycarbonate

When you bottom out a key (press it all the way down), the switch slam stops against the "Plate." The material of this plate defines the harshness of that impact. For repetitive clickers, this choice affects not just sound, but stamina.

Rigid Plates: Brass and Steel

Characteristics: Extremely stiff, heavy, and resonant. High-pitched signature.

For Clickers: Generally NOT recommended. The abrupt stop when bottoming out sends shockwaves back into your finger joints. Over thousands of clicks, this can accelerate fatigue.

Standard Plates: Aluminum

Characteristics: The middle ground. Reasonably stiff, neutral sound.

For Clickers: A safe bet. It provides a consistent feel without being overly fatiguing, but doesn't offer any "cushion."

Flexible Plates: Polycarbonate (PC) and POM

Characteristics: Plastic materials that flex under pressure. Deep, thocky sound.

For Clickers: Highly Recommended. When you are spamming the spacebar in a frenzy, a flexible plate acts like a shock absorber. It cushions the bottom-out impact, making the experience softer on your fingertips and joints. It creates a "bouncy" typing feel that can help with rhythm.

FR4 (Fiberglass)

The material circuit boards are made of. It sits between Aluminum and PC in terms of stiffness. A fantastic all-rounder popular in the custom scene.

Pro Tip: If you are suffering from finger pain, try switching to a Polycarbonate plate or a "Gasket Mounted" keyboard, which uses foam gaskets to isolate the plate from the case entirely.
